The NHS Business Services Authority (NHSBSA) has a legal and moral duty to make its services, websites, documents and digital products accessible.

Accessibility is not just about meeting standards. It's about making sure people can use our services without barriers.

## Think about accessibility from the start

In the UK, around 1 in 5 people have a disability. This may be:
- visual
- hearing
- motor (affecting movement)
- cognitive (affecting memory or understanding)

Accessibility is for everyone. Anyone's ability to use a service can change depending on their situation. For example:
- location - a noisy environment or poor internet connection
- health - illness, fatigue or temporary injury
- equipment - mobile devices or older browsers

Designing with accessibility in mind from the start will help you:
- make sure that nobody is excluded
- identify problems early, when they are quicker and cheaper to fix

Get guidance from GOV.UK on [making your service accessible](https://www.gov.uk/service-manual/helping-people-to-use-your-service/making-your-service-accessible-an-introduction).

## Accessibility law

Accessibility is a part of several laws and standards the NHSBSA must follow. Each of the standards feed into the [Equality Act 2010](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/equality-act-2010-guidance) and the [Public Sector Equality Duty](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/public-sector-equality-duty).

If your service runs in a browser, it will need to meet the [Public Sector Bodies Accessibility Regulations 2018 (PSBAR)](https://accessibility-manual.dwp.gov.uk/accessibility-law/the-public-sector-bodies-accessibility-regulations-2018).

These regulations apply to both public-facing and internal services.

## How to meet the regulations

To meet legal requirements, your service must:
- meet the latest published version of the [Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)](https://accessibility-manual.dwp.gov.uk/accessibility-law/web-content-accessibility-guidelines).
- publish a compliant [accessibility statement](https://accessibility-manual.dwp.gov.uk/accessibility-law/accessibility-statements)

### The Government accessibility requirements

To meet government service standards, you must also:
- include people with access needs in user research
- test with [common assistive technologies](https://www.gov.uk/service-manual/technology/testing-with-assistive-technologies)

### Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)

WCAG is a set of success criteria used to assess accessibility. The current version is [WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/).

To meet regulations, your service must meet Level AA.

The criteria are grouped into four principles:

- **Perceivable** – users can perceive the content (for example, text alternatives for images)
- **Operable** – users can interact with the service (for example, using a keyboard)
- **Understandable** – content is clear and predictable
- **Robust** – content works with different devices and assistive technologies

You can use these resources to understand WCAG:
- [Accessibility testing guide - GOV.UK](https://github.com/alphagov/wcag-primer/wiki)
- [WCAG explorer - Department for Education](https://accessibility.education.gov.uk/guidelines/wcag/explorer)
- [How to meet WCAG (Quick reference) - W3C](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/quickref)

## Accessibility statements

You must publish an accessibility statement for your service.

The statement must explain:
- how accessible the service is
- any known accessibility issues
- how users can request alternative formats
- how users can report problems

It must also include a compliance status:
- fully compliant – meets all WCAG Level A and AA criteria
- partially compliant – some criteria are not met
- not compliant – many criteria are not met

## Our users?
Our users have a wide range of needs and use different devices and assistive technologies.

Some access needs are not visible.

You should carry out user research to understand how people use your service and where barriers exist.

Find out [how many people have a disability or condition that might impact their ability to use your service](https://accessibility.education.gov.uk/app/how-many-people/10000).

## What we expect from suppliers
When we build or buy digital products, suppliers must:
- meet WCAG 2.2 Level AA as a minimum
- provide evidence of accessibility testing
- publish an accessibility statement
- provide a clear, time-bound roadmap to fix issues

Accessibility must be considered across the whole user journey, including:
- content
- documents
- outputs generated by the system

Updates must be designed, developed and tested for accessibility before release.

If the product includes authoring tools, suppliers must support the creation of accessible content.

### Accessibility overlays

Some companies offer “accessibility overlays” or “widgets” that claim to make a service Some tools claim to fix accessibility issues using overlays or widgets.

These tools do not make a service compliant with accessibility law.

They often introduce new barriers and do not fix underlying problems.

Accessibility must be built into the service, not added afterwards.

The NHSBSA does not accept overlays as a solution.
